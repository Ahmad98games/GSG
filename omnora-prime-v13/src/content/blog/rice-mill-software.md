# Rice Mill Management Software Pakistan — Complete 2026 Guide

Rice milling is one of Pakistan's largest agricultural processing industries. Punjab alone has over 2,000 registered rice mills, processing millions of maunds of paddy annually. The major rice-growing districts — Sheikhupura, Hafizabad, Gujranwala, Rahim Yar Khan, and Larkana — collectively contribute to Pakistan being one of the world's top five rice exporters.

Yet despite this enormous scale and commercial significance, most Pakistani rice mills still track their core operations using a combination of paper registers, physical ledger books, and basic Excel spreadsheets. The result is substantial: leakage, disputes, missed payments, and operational inefficiencies that quietly drain profitability.

This guide covers every aspect of modern rice mill management — from paddy procurement to final dealer payment — and explains how a purpose-built digital system transforms operations.

## Part 1: The Business Complexity of Rice Milling

### Understanding the Full Value Chain

A rice mill's business is more complex than it initially appears. The full operational chain includes:

**Paddy Procurement:**
- Multiple farmers selling different varieties (Basmati 386, Super Kernel, IRRI-6, etc.)
- Different moisture content levels affecting price and yield
- Daily fluctuating Mandi rates for each variety
- Multiple payment arrangements: cash on delivery, credit to Arthis (brokers), advance payments to contracted farmers

**Milling Operations:**
- Paddy dehusking (removing the outer husk layer)
- Whitening/polishing to different grades (Extra Long, Long, Short)
- Sorting and grading by broken percentage
- Bagging in standard Boris (50 kg sacks) or custom export packaging

**Sales and Distribution:**
- Local wholesale dealers buying on credit
- Rice brokers arranging export shipments
- Direct sales to hotels, restaurants, and institutions
- Traders buying surplus capacity during peak season

**Inventory Management:**
- Paddy stock (raw input)
- Husk (byproduct — sold as fuel or fertilizer)
- Bran (byproduct — sold for oil extraction)
- Milled rice stock in various grades
- Packaging materials (sacks, bags, threads)

Managing all of these streams with accuracy requires a system designed specifically for this industry — not a generic accounting package.

## Part 2: Where Manual Systems Break Down

### The Paddy Procurement Gap

Paddy procurement happens fast, especially during harvest season (October–December for Basmati, March–April for IRRI varieties). During peak season, a mid-sized rice mill may procure 200–500 maunds of paddy per day from dozens of different farmers.

**Problems with manual tracking:**
- Weighing slips are handwritten and easy to dispute
- Moisture content readings are recorded separately from weight, creating reconciliation gaps
- Payments to Arthis (brokers) vs. direct payments to farmers become difficult to track
- Paddy variety and grade data gets mixed up when multiple lots arrive simultaneously

The practical result: at the end of the season, the mill owner cannot accurately reconcile how much paddy they procured, from whom, at what rate, and what they still owe. This creates significant financial exposure.

### The Milling Yield Mystery

Milling yield — the ratio of milled rice output to paddy input — is the single most important profitability metric in rice milling. A typical yield for Basmati rice is 65–70%: for every 100 maunds of paddy, you should get 65–70 maunds of milled rice, with the remainder being husk and bran.

When actual yield falls below this expected range, there are three possible explanations:
1. **The paddy had higher moisture content** than measured, meaning it lost more weight during drying and milling
2. **The machine calibration** is off, producing more breakage than expected (which reduces the value of the milled rice)
3. **Pilferage** — paddy or milled rice being removed from the premises without authorization

Without a real-time, batch-level yield tracking system, it's impossible to know which of these is happening. Most mills discover yield problems during physical stock audits — by which point significant losses have already occurred.

### The Dealer Credit Maze

Rice is almost universally sold on credit in Pakistan's domestic trade. A typical payment cycle:
- Dealer takes delivery of 50 Boris (2,500 kg) of rice
- Invoice is issued
- Dealer makes partial payment within 7–15 days
- Balance is settled over 30–45 days, sometimes longer

A rice mill dealing with 30–50 active dealers simultaneously has hundreds of outstanding invoices at any given time. Manual ledger books (Khata) track these balances — but they're easily miscalculated, hard to share with the dealer for verification, and impossible to analyze for trends.

Common outcomes of poor dealer credit tracking:
- Overdue balances accumulate because no one is monitoring them systematically
- Dealers dispute invoice amounts with no clear documentation
- The mill extends more credit to a dealer who is already seriously overdue, because nobody checked the balance before accepting the new order
- Year-end reconciliation reveals large write-offs that could have been prevented with better real-time tracking

## Part 3: Key Features of Proper Rice Mill Software

### Feature 1: Native Pakistani Units

Any software designed for Pakistani rice mills must support:
- **Maund (40 kg)** — the primary weight unit for paddy procurement and milling
- **Kg** — for precise measurements and moisture-corrected weights
- **Bori/Bag (50 kg)** — for milled rice inventory and sales
- **Quintal (100 kg)** — used in some export contexts

Conversion between units should be automatic and transparent. A system that forces you to convert to grams or works only in metric kilograms adds an unnecessary cognitive burden to every entry.

### Feature 2: Mandi Rate Integration

Mandi rates for paddy varieties change daily based on:
- National harvest projections
- Export demand from Middle East and African markets
- Currency exchange rates (for export mills)
- Local supply/demand in specific Mandi markets

A good system lets the mill record daily Mandi rates for each variety they procure. This rate history serves multiple purposes:
- Verify that procurement prices were at or below market at the time of purchase
- Calculate the theoretical value of current paddy stock at current market prices
- Analyze procurement cost trends over seasons

### Feature 3: Batch-Level Yield Tracking

Each processing batch should be recorded as:
- **Input:** Paddy variety, quantity (maunds), moisture content at intake
- **Process:** Date milled, machine used, duration
- **Output:** Milled rice by grade and quantity, husk quantity, bran quantity, broken/kani quantity
- **Calculated yield:** Automatically computed and compared against expected yield

When actual yield falls outside the expected range, the system generates an alert. This allows the mill manager to investigate the specific batch immediately, before the discrepancy grows.

### Feature 4: Complete Procurement Ledger

Every paddy purchase should create a procurement record with:
- Farmer/Arth name and contact
- Variety and grade
- Quantity (maunds)
- Rate per maund
- Moisture adjustment (if any)
- Total amount payable
- Payment method and status
- Linked payments against the procurement

The system should provide a clear view of: how much paddy is currently on-hand, from whom it was procured, and what payment obligations are still outstanding.

### Feature 5: Dealer Khata Management

Each dealer gets their own Khata (ledger account) showing:
- All invoices issued (with amounts, dates, and due dates)
- All payments received
- Running outstanding balance
- Days since each invoice was issued
- Credit limit and current utilization

From a single dashboard, the mill manager can see all dealer outstanding balances sorted by aging — oldest invoices first. This makes it immediately clear which dealers need follow-up.
