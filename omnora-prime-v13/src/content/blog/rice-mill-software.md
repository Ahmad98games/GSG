# Rice Mill Management Software — Complete Guide

Rice milling is one of Pakistan's largest agricultural processing industries. Punjab alone has over 2,000 registered rice mills, processing millions of maunds of paddy annually. The major rice-growing districts — Sheikhupura, Hafizabad, Gujranwala, Rahim Yar Khan, and Larkana — collectively make Pakistan one of the world's top five rice exporters.

Yet despite this massive scale, most Pakistani rice mills still track their core operations using paper registers, physical ledger books, and basic Excel spreadsheets. This leads to stock leakage, payment arguments, and missing details that quietly drain your profits.

This guide covers every aspect of modern rice mill management — from buying paddy (raw rice crop) to final dealer payments — and explains how a simple digital system transforms your mill.

## Part 1: The Business Complexity of Rice Milling

### Understanding the Full Process

A rice mill's business is more complex than it appears. The full operational steps include:

**Paddy Buying:**
- Multiple farmers selling different varieties (Basmati 386, Super Kernel, IRRI-6, etc.)
- Different moisture levels affecting the price and final weight
- Daily fluctuating Mandi market rates for each variety
- Multiple payment setups: cash on delivery, credit to Arthis (brokers), and advance payments to farmers

**Milling Operations:**
- Paddy dehusking (removing the outer skin)
- Whitening and polishing to different grades (Extra Long, Long, Short)
- Sorting and grading by broken percentage (Kani)
- Packing in standard Boris (50 kg bags) or custom export sacks

**Sales and Distribution:**
- Local wholesale dealers buying on credit
- Rice brokers arranging export shipments
- Direct sales to hotels, restaurants, and shops

**Inventory Management:**
- Paddy stock (raw input)
- Husk (leftover byproduct — sold as fuel or fertilizer)
- Bran (leftover byproduct — sold for oil extraction)
- Milled rice stock in various grades
- Packing materials (bags, sacks, thread)

Managing all of these streams with accuracy requires a system designed specifically for this industry — not a generic computer accounting program.

## Part 2: Where Manual Systems Break Down

### The Paddy Buying Gap

Paddy buying happens fast, especially during harvest season (October–December for Basmati, March–April for IRRI). During peak season, a mid-sized rice mill may buy 200 to 500 maunds of paddy per day from dozens of different farmers.

**Problems with manual tracking:**
- Weighing slips are handwritten and easy to lose or dispute
- Moisture readings are recorded separately from weight, creating matching errors
- Payments to Arthis (brokers) versus direct payments to farmers become difficult to track
- Paddy variety and grade details get mixed up when multiple trucks arrive at the same time

The result: at the end of the season, the mill owner cannot accurately check how much paddy they bought, from whom, at what rate, and what they still owe. This creates major financial risks.

### The Milling Yield Mystery

Milling yield — the ratio of clean milled rice output to raw paddy input — is the single most important metric for your profit. A typical yield for Basmati rice is 65 to 70%: for every 100 maunds of raw paddy, you should get 65 to 70 maunds of milled rice, with the remainder being husk and bran.

When the actual yield falls below this range, there are three possible explanations:
1. **The paddy had higher moisture** than measured, meaning it lost more weight during drying and milling.
2. **The milling machine calibration** is off, producing more broken rice than expected (which reduces the value of the rice).
3. **Theft** — paddy or milled rice being taken from the warehouse without authorization.

Without a real-time, batch-by-batch yield tracking system, it is impossible to know which of these is happening. Most mills only discover yield problems during physical stock counts, by which point major losses have already occurred.

### The Dealer Credit Maze

Rice is almost always sold on credit in Pakistan. A typical payment cycle:
- A dealer takes delivery of 50 Boris (2,500 kg) of rice.
- An invoice (bill) is issued.
- The dealer makes a partial payment within 7 to 15 days.
- The remaining balance is settled over 30 to 45 days.

A rice mill dealing with 30 to 50 active dealers has hundreds of outstanding invoices at any given time. Manual ledger books (Khata) track these balances, but they are easily miscalculated, hard to share with the dealer for verification, and impossible to analyze for trends.

Common outcomes of poor credit tracking:
- Overdue balances accumulate because no one is monitoring them.
- Dealers dispute bill amounts because there are no clear logs.
- The mill extends more credit to a dealer who is already seriously overdue, because nobody checked their balance before accepting the new order.
- Year-end books reveal large unpaid debts that could have been prevented with real-time tracking.

## Part 3: Key Features of Proper Rice Mill Software

### Feature 1: Native Pakistani Units

Any software designed for Pakistani rice mills must support:
- **Maund (40 kg)** — the primary weight unit for paddy buying and milling
- **Kg** — for precise measurements and moisture-corrected weights
- **Bori/Bag (50 kg)** — for milled rice stock and sales
- **Quintal (100 kg)** — used in some export contexts

Conversion between these units should be automatic. A system that forces you to convert to grams or works only in standard metric kilograms adds unnecessary mental effort to every entry.

### Feature 2: Mandi Rate Integration

Mandi rates for paddy change daily based on:
- National harvest projections
- Export demand
- Local supply and demand in specific Mandi markets

A good system lets the mill record daily Mandi rates for each variety they buy. This rate history helps you:
- Verify that purchase prices were at or below market at the time of purchase.
- Calculate the current value of your paddy stock at current market prices.
- Analyze buying cost trends over seasons.

### Feature 3: Batch-by-Batch Yield Tracking

Each processing batch should be recorded in the system:
- **Input:** Paddy variety, quantity (maunds), moisture content at intake.
- **Process:** Date milled, machine used.
- **Output:** Milled rice by grade and quantity, husk quantity, bran quantity, broken (kani) quantity.
- **Calculated yield:** Automatically computed and compared against expected yield.

When the actual yield falls outside the expected range, the system generates an alert. This allows the mill manager to investigate the specific machine or batch immediately.

### Feature 4: Complete Buying Ledger

Every paddy purchase should create a record showing:
- Farmer or broker name and contact
- Variety and grade
- Quantity (maunds)
- Rate per maund
- Moisture adjustment (if any)
- Total amount payable
- Payment method and status

The system should provide a clear view of: how much paddy is currently on-hand, from whom it was bought, and what payments are still outstanding.

### Feature 5: Dealer Khata Management

Each dealer gets their own Khata (ledger account) showing:
- All bills issued (with amounts, dates, and due dates)
- All payments received
- Running outstanding balance
- How old each unpaid bill is
- Credit limit and current utilization

From a single screen, the mill manager can see all dealer outstanding balances sorted by age — oldest bills first. This makes it immediately clear which dealers need follow-up.
